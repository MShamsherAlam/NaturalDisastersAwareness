What is an Earthquake?

An earthquake (also known as a quake, tremor or temblor) is the perceptible shaking of the surface of the Earth, resulting from the sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust that creates seismic waves. Earthquakes can be violent enough to

  • 1. toss people around and destroy whole cities.
  • 2. alter the surface of the Earth, thrusting up cliffs and opening great cracks in the ground
  • 3. collapse of buildings and other man-made structures.
  • 4. initiate landslides, snow avalanches, tsunamis (giant sea waves) and volcanic eruptions.

Interesting fact: Earthquake vibrations travel very fast, up to 14 kilometres per second. The fastest seismic waves take less than 20 minutes to reach the other side of the earth, a distance of almost 13,000 kilometres!

What is the Richter Magnitude Scale?

The Richter magnitude scale, also known as the local magnitude (M) scale, assigns a number to quantify the amount of seismic energy released by an earthquake. It is a base-10 logarithmic scale.

Can we predict an earthquake?

  • 1. Despite considerable research predictions cannot yet be made to a specific day or month.
  • 2. However, for well-understood faults the probability that a segment may rupture during the next few decades can be estimated.
  • 3. Animals display unusual behavior some tens of seconds prior to a quake.
  • 4. Earthquake warning systems have been developed that can provide regional notification of an earthquake in progress, but before the ground surface has begun to move, potentially allowing people within the system's range to seek shelter before the earthquake's impact is felt.

What to do before an earthquake!

  • 1.First take a deep breath!
  • 2.Repair deep plaster cracks in ceilings and foundations. Get expert advice if there are signs of structural defects.
  • 3.Fasten shelves securely to walls.
  • 4.Anchor overhead lighting fixtures to the ceiling.
  • 5.Follow BIS codes relevant to your area for building standards.
  • 6.Repair defective electrical wiring and leaky gas connections. These are potential fire risks.
  • 7.Know emergency telephone numbers (such as those of doctors, hospitals, the police, etc)
  • 8.Educate yourself and family members.
  • 9.In case family members are separated from one another during an earthquake (a real possibility during the day when are at work and children are at school), develop a plan for reuniting after the disaster.
  • 10.Ask an out-of-state relative or friend to serve as the 'family contact' after the disaster; it is often easier to call long distance. Make sure everyone in the family knows the name, address, and phone number of the contact person.

What NOT to do before an earthquake.

  • 1.Don't Panic!
  • 2.Do not ignore any cracks in ceilings and foundations in your home.
  • 3.Make sure you dont have a complex emergency route at the times of real situation.

What to do during an earthquake!

  • 1. Stay as safe as possible during an earthquake.
  • 2. Be aware that some earthquakes are actually foreshocks and a larger earthquake might occur.
  • 3. Minimize your movements to a few steps that reach a nearby safe place and stay indoors until the shaking has stopped and you are sure exiting is safe.

What to do when you are indoors!

  • 1. Drop to the ground. The drop, cover, and hold on technique is the cousin of the famous "stop, drop and roll" for fires.
  • 2. Take cover. Get under a sturdy table or other piece of furniture. If possible, stay away from glass, windows, outside doors and walls, and anything that could fall, such as lighting fixtures or furniture.
  • 3. Hold on. The ground may be shaking and debris could be falling. Hold on to whatever surface or platform you've gotten under and wait for the shaking to subside.
  • 4. If you find yourself in bed while an earthquake strikes, stay there. Hold on and protect your head with a pillow, unless you are under a heavy light fixture that could fall. In that case, move to the nearest safe place.
  • 5. Stay inside until shaking stops and it is safe to go outside. Research suggests that many injuries occur when people inside buildings attempt to move to a different location inside the building or try to leave.

What NOT to do when you are indoors!

  • 1. Do not Run outside. You're more likely to get injured trying to get out of the building than staying put.
  • 2. Do not Head for a doorway. Hiding under a doorway is a myth. You're safer under a table than you are under a doorway, especially in modern houses.
  • 3. Do not Run to another room to get under a table or other piece of furniture.
  • 4. Do not use elevators. The power can go out, causing you to be trapped. Your best bet is to use the stairwell if it's free.

What to do when you are outside!

  • 1. Stay there.
  • 2. Move away from buildings, streetlights, and utility wires.
  • 3. Once in the open, stay there until the shaking stops. The greatest danger exists directly outside buildings, at exits and alongside exterior walls.

What do do when you are inside a moving vehicle

  • 1. Stop as quickly as safety permits and stay in the vehicle. Avoid stopping near or under buildings, trees, overpasses, and utility wires.
  • 2. Proceed cautiously once the earthquake has stopped. Avoid roads, bridges, or ramps that might have been damaged by the earthquake.

What to do when you are trapped under debris!

  • 1. Do not light a match.
  • 2. Do not move about or kick up dust.
  • 3. Cover your mouth with a handkerchief or clothing.
  • 4. Tap on a pipe or wall so rescuers can locate you. Use a whistle if one is available.
  • 5. Shout only as a last resort. Shouting can cause you to inhale dangerous amounts of dust.

What to do after the earthquake is over!

  • 1. Keep calm, switch on the radio/TV and obey any instructions you hear on it.
  • 2. Keep away from beaches and low banks of rivers. Huge waves may sweep in.
  • 3. Be prepared to expect aftershocks.
  • 4. Turn off the water, gas and electricity.
  • 5. If there is a fire, try to put it out. If you cannot, call the fire brigade.
  • 6. Immediately clean up any inflammable products that may have spilled (alcohol, paint, etc).
  • 7. If you know that people have been buried, tell the rescue teams
  • 8. Avoid places where there are loose electric wires and do not touch any metal object in contact with them.
  • 9. If your home is badly damaged, you will have to leave it. Collect water containers, food, and ordinary and special medicines (for persons with heart complaints, diabetes, etc.)

What NOT to do after the earthquake is over!

  • 1. Do not smoke and do not light matches or use a cigarette lighter.
  • 2. Do not turn on switches. There may be gas leaks or short-circuits.
  • 3. Use a torch.
  • 4. Do not rush and do not worsen the situation of injured persons or your own situation.
  • 5. Do not drink water from open containers without having examined it and filtered it through a sieve, a filter or an ordinary clean cloth.
  • 6. If people are seriously injured, do not move them unless they are in danger.
  • 7. Do not re-enter badly damaged buildings and do not go near damaged structures.

What is a Tornado?

A tornado is a violent and destructive storm in which powerful winds move around a central point that is in contact with both the surface of the earth and a cumulonimbus cloud. They are often referred to as twisters or cyclones. They can

  • 1. destroy large buildings
  • 2. lift 20-ton railroad cars from their tracks
  • 3. drive a blade of straw through a telephone pole.

Interesting Fact: The most extreme tornadoes can attain wind speeds of more than 300 miles per hour (480 km/h), stretch more than two miles (3 km) across, and stay on the ground for dozens of miles (more than 100 km).

Types of Tornadoes

1. Multiple vortex

A multiple-vortex tornado is a type of tornado in which two or more columns of spinning air rotate around a common center. A multi-vortex structure can occur in almost any circulation, but is very often observed in intense tornadoes.

2. Waterspout

A waterspout is defined by the National Weather Service as a tornado over water. However, researchers typically distinguish "fair weather" waterspouts from tornadic waterspouts. Fair weather waterspouts are less severe but far more common, and are similar to dust devils and landspouts.In contrast, tornadic waterspouts are stronger tornadoes over water.

3. Landspout

A landspout, or dust-tube tornado, is a tornado not associated with a mesocyclone. The name stems from their characterization as a "fair weather waterspout on land". Waterspouts and landspouts share many defining characteristics, including relative weakness, short lifespan, and a small, smooth condensation funnel which often does not reach the surface.usually weaker than classic tornadoes, they can produce strong winds which could cause serious damage.

4. Gustnado

A gustnado, or gust front tornado, is a small, vertical swirl associated with a gust front or downburst.

5. Dust devil

A dust devil resembles a tornado in that it is a vertical swirling column of air. However, they form under clear skies and are no stronger than the weakest tornadoes.

6. Fire whirls

Small-scale, tornado-like circulations can occur near any intense surface heat source. Those that occur near intense wildfires are called fire whirls.

7. Steam devils

A steam devil is a rotating updraft that involves steam or smoke. Steam devils are very rare.

Predicting a Tornado!

Predicting tornadoes has come a long way since the first one was issued 65 years ago. Today, forecasters are trying to find ways to gather even more atmospheric data to give those in harm's way as much warning as possible.
The National Weather Service's Storm Prediction Center issues general severe weather outlooks and watches.

  • 1. The severe weather forecast process begins with the Convective Outlook. This tells you where you can expect both severe and non-severe thunderstorms to occur around the country.
  • 2. Areas of possible severe thunderstorms are labeled slight, moderate, or high risk depending upon the coverage and intensity of expected storms in a region.
  • 3. These are issued for today, tomorrow, the day after tomorrow, and the rest of the week.
  • 5. If a severe thunderstorm appears imminent, or likely to occur in the next several hours, the NWS will issue a severe thunderstorm or tornado Watch.
  • 6. Once a watch is issued, forecasters in the threat area closely monitor radar imagery and spotter reports to issue the appropriate severe thunderstorm and tornado warnings.
  • 7. When a tornado appears imminent, local NWS offices issue a tornado Warning, which means a tornado has been spotted or that Doppler radar indicates a thunderstorm circulation that can spawn a tornado.
  • 8. When a tornado warning is issued for your town or county, take immediate safety precautions.

What to do before a tornado strikes?

Sometimes tornadoes do not give weather readers much time to get people prepared to take cover. Here are a few things to do in preparation, especially if you live in a tornado prone area:

  • 1. Always be aware of the safer places (Refuge areas) you can go to.
  • 2. If there is enough time, grab a few first aid items and stock up on water and some emergency supplies, that can take you a few days if things get very bad.
  • 3. Try to keep in touch with your local weather station, and look out for dark clouds and thunderstorms.
  • 4. Be aware of the weather in your town and the suggested actions you can do to keep safe.

What to do after hearing a tornado warning?

You're in your house, your car, maybe your mobile home, and you've just heard a tornado warning announced for your area. What should you do now? Just as importantly, what shouldn't you do? Here are some dos and don'ts.

What to DO during a tornado?

  • 1. Take shelter area such as a safe room, basement, storm cellar or the lowest building level.
  • 2. Stay away from windows.
  • 3. Listen to a NOAA Weather Radio, regular radio or television for tornado updates.
  • 4. Turn off major electrical items.
  • 5. Do not try to outrun a tornado
  • 6. use flashlights.
  • 7. Use battery-powered devices, in case the electricity goes out.
  • 8. if you happen to live in a trailer, Get out.
  • 9. If youre in a car and you're not sure which way the tornado is moving, get out of the vehicle.
  • 10. Head to the lowest floor of a nearby building or storm shelter.
  • 11. If youre in a office building, hospital, skyscraper or other tall building, go to an enclosed room with no windows at the center of the building on the lowest floor.
  • 12. If you're outside with no shelter, lie flat in a ditch or depression and cover your head with your hands. Be aware of the potential for flooding.

What not to do during a tornado?

  • 1. Don't use your car as shelter.
  • 2. Do not try to outrun a tornado.
  • 3. Dont Ignore the warnings: When we ignore the warnings, is when we become victims.
  • 4. Don't sightsee.
  • 5. Don't go outside.
  • 6. Don't open the windows in your home.
  • 7. Don't use elevators.
  • 8. Don't light candles, even after the storm has passed. Ruptured gas lines can create a fire hazard.

Of course this article can not cover all the possible do's and don'ts, but it does serve to make you the reader more aware of the potential dangers and risks associated with tornadoes. The sirens and warnings are there for a reason, to keep you as safe as possible.

What to do after a tornado is over?

  • 1. Keep your family together after the tornado.
  • 2. Be careful when getting out of your shelter as damaged objects and structures may fall.
  • 3. Wear safety garments when walking and working through debris, as there could be broken glasses, exposed nails and other dangerous chemicals.
  • 4. Do not touch power lines and objects in water puddles as there may be live electrical wires around.
  • 5. If you have to clean up your home, make sure that you are wearing safety gear and are well aware of the dangers.
  • 6. Keep records, notes, photos of broken items, in case your insurance company needs them.
  • 7. Remain calm as you wait for more information and instructions from emergency crews or local officials.

What is a Tsunami?

A tsunami also known as a seismic sea wave, is a series of giant waves in a water body caused by the displacement of a large volume of water, generally in an ocean or a large lake.The word tsunami comes from the Japanese words tsu and nami meaning "harbor and wave."

  • 1. Earthquakes, volcanic eruptions and other underwater explosions (including detonations of underwater nuclear devices), landslides, glacier calvings, meteorite impacts and other disturbances above or below water all have the potential to generate a tsunami.
  • 2. Out in the depths of the ocean, tsunami waves do not dramatically increase in height. But as the waves travel inland, they build up to higher and higher heights as the depth of the ocean decreases.
  • 3. The speed of tsunami waves depends on ocean depth rather than the distance from the source of the wave.
  • 4. Tsunami waves may travel as fast as jet planes over deep waters, only slowing down when reaching shallow waters.
  • 5. Although a tsunami cannot be prevented, the impact of a tsunami can be mitigated through community preparedness, timely warnings, and effective response.

Interesting Fact: Tsunamis are very rare. There are roughly six major tsunamis each century.

Predicting a Tsunami!

  • 1. A tsunami cannot be precisely predicted, even if the magnitude and location of an earthquake is known.
  • 2. Computer models can predict tsunami arrival, usually within minutes of the arrival time. However, there are some warning signs of an impending tsunami, and automated systems can provide warnings immediately after an earthquake in time to save lives.

Warnings!

  • 1. A tsunami watch is issued to alert emergency management officials and the public of an event which may later impact the watch area.
  • 2. A tsunami advisory is issued when a tsunami with the potential to generate strong currents or waves dangerous to those in or very near the water is imminent or expected.
  • 3. A tsunami warning is issued when a tsunami with the potential to generate widespread inundation is imminent or expected.
  • 4. Warnings alert the public that dangerous coastal flooding accompanied by powerful currents is possible and may continue for several hours after initial arrival.
  • 5. Warnings alert emergency management officials to take action for the entire tsunami hazard zone.
  • 6. Appropriate actions to be taken by local officials may include the evacuation of low-lying coastal areas, and the repositioning of ships to deep waters when there is time to safely do so.
  • 7. Warnings may be updated, adjusted geographically, downgraded, or canceled.
  • 8. Regions with a high tsunami risk typically use tsunami warning systems to warn the population before the wave reaches land.
  • 9. Along the United States west coast, in addition to sirens, warnings signs indicating evacuation routes are sent on television and radio via the National Weather Service, using the Emergency Alert System warning.

What to DO before a Tsunami?

The following are things you can do to protect yourself, your family and your property from the effects of a tsunami:

  • 1. Plan evacuation routes from your home, school, workplace, or any other place you could be where tsunamis present a risk.
  • 2. You should be able to reach your safe location on foot within 15 minutes.
  • 3. If the school evacuation plan requires you to pick your children up from school or from another location.
  • 4. Knowing your community's warning systems and disaster plans, including evacuation routes.
  • 5. Know the height of your street above sea level and the distance of your street from the coast or other high-risk waters. Evacuation orders may be based on these numbers.
  • 6. If you are a tourist, familiarize yourself with local tsunami evacuation protocols. You may be able to safely evacuate to the third floor and higher in reinforced concrete hotel structures.
  • 7. If an earthquake occurs and you are in a coastal area, turn on your radio to learn if there is a tsunami warning.

What you should keep in mind when you are visiting an area at risk from tsunami?

  • 1. Check with the hotel or campground operators for tsunami evacuation information and find out what the warning system is for tsunamis.
  • 2. One of the early warning signals of a tsunami is that the sea water recedes several metres, exposing fish on shallow waters or on the beaches. If you see the sea water receding, you must immediately leave the beach and go to higher ground far away from the beach.
  • 3. Protect Your Property.
  • 4. You should avoid building or living in buildings within 200 meters of the high tide coastline.
  • 5. These areas are more likely to experience damage from tsunamis, strong winds, or coastal storms.
  • 6. Make a list of items to bring inside in the event of a tsunami.
  • 7. A list will help you remember anything that can be swept away by tsunami water.
  • 8. Elevate coastal homes.
  • 9. Most tsunami waves are less than 3 meters. Elevating your house will help reduce damage to your property from most tsunamis.
  • 10. Take precautions to prevent flooding.
  • 11. Have an engineer check your home and advise ways to make it more resistant to tsunami water.
  • 12. There may be ways to divert waves away from your property. Improperly built walls could make your situation worse. Consult with a professional for advice.
  • 13. Ensure that any outbuildings, pastures, or corrals are protected in the same way as your home. When installing or changing fence lines, consider placing them in such a way that your animals are able to move to higher ground in the event of a tsunami.

What to do after you get a Tsunami warning?

  • 1. Follow the evacuation order issued by authorities and evacuate immediately.
  • 2. If you are in a dangerous area, immediately turn off the water, gas, and electricity.
  • 3. Move inland to higher ground immediately. Pick areas 100 feet (30 meters) above sea level or go as far as 2 miles (3 kilometers) inland, away from the coastline.
  • 4. The upper floors of high reinforced concrete hotels can provide a safe place to find refuge if you cannot move inland to higher ground quickly.
  • 5. Stay away from the beach.
  • 6. If there is noticeable recession in water away from the shoreline this is nature's tsunami warning and it should be heeded. You should move away immediately.
  • 7. Save yourself - not your possessions.
  • 8. Remember to help your neighbors who may require special assistance - infants, elderly people, and individuals with access or functional needs.
  • 9. If you are on a boat do not return to port.

What NOT to do during Tsunami?

  • 1. Never go down to the beach to watch a tsunami come in.
  • 2. Do not stay in Homes and small buildings located in low-lying coastal areas.

What to do after Tsunami is gone?

  • 1. Listen to a portable radio to learn when it is safe to return home.
  • 2. All buildings should be checked for gas leaks and electrical shorts before anyone enters.
  • 3. Do not return to low lying areas until the tsunami threat has passed and the "all clear" is announced.
  • 4. You should continue using a Weather Radio or staying tuned to a Coast Guard emergency frequency station or a local radio or television station for updated emergency information.
  • 5. The Tsunami may have damaged roads, bridges, or other places that may be unsafe.
  • 6. Check yourself for injuries and get first aid if necessary before helping injured or trapped persons.
  • 7. If someone needs to be rescued, call professionals with the right equipment to help.
  • 8. Help people who require special assistance Infants, elderly people, those without transportation, large families who may need additional help in an emergency situation, people with disabilities, and the people who care for them.
  • 9. Avoid disaster areas.
  • 10. Your presence might hamper rescue and other emergency operations and put you at further risk from the residual effects of floods, such as contaminated water, crumbled roads, landslides, mudflows, and other hazards.
  • 11. Use the telephone only for emergency calls.Telephone lines are frequently overwhelmed in disaster situations. They need to be clear for emergency calls to get through.
  • 12. Stay out of a building if water remains around it.Tsunami water, like floodwater, can undermine foundations, causing buildings to sink, floors to crack, or walls to collapse.
  • 13. When re-entering buildings or homes, use extreme caution.Tsunami-driven floodwater may have damaged buildings where you least expect it. Carefully watch every step you take.
  • 14. Wear long pants, a long-sleeved shirt, and sturdy shoes.The most common injury following a disaster is cut feet.
  • 15. Use battery-powered lanterns or flashlights when examining buildings.Battery-powered lighting is the safest and easiest to use, and it does not present a fire hazard for the user, occupants, or building. DO NOT USE CANDLES.
  • 16. Examine walls, floors, doors, staircases, and windows to make sure that the building is not in danger of collapsing. Inspect foundations for cracks or other damage. Cracks and damage to a foundation can render a building uninhabitable.
  • 17. Look for fire hazards.Under the earthquake action there may be broken or leaking gas lines, and under the tsunami flooded electrical circuits, or submerged furnaces or electrical appliances.
  • 18. Check for gas leaks.If you smell gas or hear a blowing or hissing noise, open a window and get everyone outside quickly. Turn off the gas using the outside main valve if you can, and call the gas company from a neighbours home. If you turn off the gas for any reason, it must be turned back on by a professional.
  • 19. Look for electrical system damage. If you see sparks or broken or frayed wires, or if you smell burning insulation, turn off the electricity at the main fuse box or circuit breaker. If you have to step in water to get to the fuse box or circuit breaker, call an electrician first for advice. Electrical equipment should be checked and dried before being returned to service.
  • 20. Check for damage to sewage and water lines. You can obtain safe water from undamaged water heaters or by melting ice cubes that were made before the tsunami hit. Turn off the main water valve before draining water from these sources. Use tap water only if local health officials advise it is safe.
  • 21. Watch out for wild animals, especially poisonous snakes that may have come into buildings with the water. Use a stick to poke through debris. Tsunami floodwater flushes snakes and animals out of their homes.
  • 22. Watch for loose plaster, drywall, and ceilings that could fall.
  • 23. Take pictures of the damage, both of the building and its contents, for insurance claims. Open the windows and doors to help dry the building.
  • 24. Shovel mud before it solidifies.
  • 25. Check food supplies.
  • 26. Any food that has come in contact with floodwater may be contaminated and should be thrown out.
  • 27. Expect aftershocks. If the earthquake is of large magnitude (magnitude 8 to 9+ on the Richter scale) and located nearby, some aftershocks could be as large as magnitude 7+ and capable of generating another tsunami.
  • 28. Watch your animals closely. Keep all your animals under your direct control.

What is a Flood?

A flood is a great flowing or an overflow of a large amount of water beyond its normal limits that submerges land which is usually dry.
Flooding may occur as an overflow of water from water bodies, such as a river or lake, in which the water overtops or breaks levels, resulting in some of that water escaping its usual boundaries, or it may occur due to an accumulation of rainwater on saturated ground in an areal flood.
What floods can do?

  • 1. Flash floods can bring walls of water from 10 to 20 feet high.
  • 2. A car can be taken away in as little as 2 feet of water.
  • 3. Floods with a height of 2 meters of water can float a large vehicle even a bus.
  • 4. Floods kills animals, and others insects are introduced to affected areas, distorting the natural balance of the ecosystem.

Interesting fact: Natural flooding of river plains and deltas each year are essential for farming in many areas of the world as the waters bring nutrient rich silt deposits that create very fertile alluvial soils.

Types of flood

1. Areal
  • 1. Floods can happen on flat or low-lying areas when water is supplied by rainfall or snowmelt more rapidly than it can either infiltrate or run off.
  • 2. The excess accumulates in place, sometimes to hazardous depths.
  • 3. Surface soil can become saturated, which effectively stops infiltration, where the water table is shallow, such as a floodplain, or from intense rain from one or a series of storms.
2. Riverine (Channel)
  • 1. Floods occur in all types of river and stream channels, from the smallest ephemeral streams in humid zones to normally-dry channels in arid climates to the world's largest rivers.
  • 2. When overland flow occurs on tilled fields, it can result in a muddy flood where sediments arepicked up by run off and carried as suspended matter or bed load.
3. Estuarine and coastal

Flooding in estuaries is commonly caused by a combination of sea tidal surges caused by winds and low barometric pressure, and they may be exacerbated by high upstream river flow.

4. Urban Flooding

Urban flooding is the inundation of land or property in a built environment, particularly in more densely populated areas, caused by rainfall overwhelming the capacity of drainage systems, such as storm sewers.

5. Catastrophic

Catastrophic flooding is usually associated with major infrastructure failures such as the collapse of a dam, but they may also be caused by drainage channel modification from a landslide, earthquake or volcanic eruption. Examples include outburst floods and lahars.

Predicting floods!

Predicting floods is notoriously tricky. Sponsored by NASA, a new computer tool known as the "Global Flood Monitoring System" is improving forecasts.

  • 1. GFMS maps flood conditions worldwide, is now available online.
  • 2. GFMS works 24/7, even when there is cloud cover or other interference.
  • 3. GFMS relies on precipitation data from NASAs Earth observing satellites.
  • 4. Originally, the system relied on the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission satellite.
  • 5. Earlier this year, GFMS transitioned to the new Global Precipitation Measurement satellite, or GPM.
  • 6. Rainfall data from GPM is combined with a land surface model that incorporates vegetation cover, soil type, and terrain to determine how much water is soaking in--and how much is feeding the streamflow.

Forecasting

  • 1. Flood forecasting is the use of real-time precipitation and streamflow data in rainfall-runoff and streamflow routing models to forecast flow rates and water levels for periods ranging from a few hours to days ahead, depending on the size of the watershed or river basin.
  • 2. Flood forecasting can also make use of forecasts of precipitation in an attempt to extend the lead-time available.

Warnings!

Take Action! A Flood Warning is issued when the hazardous weather event is imminent or already happening. A Flood Warning is issued when flooding is imminent or occurring.
The task of providing warning for floods is divided into two parts:

  • 1. decisions to escalate or change the state of alertness internal to the flood warning service provider, where this may sometimes include partner organisations involved in emergency response.
  • 2. decisions to issue flood warnings to the general public.

Flood Watch!

Flood Watch = Be Aware. Conditions are right for flooding to occur in your area.
Take the following steps:

  • 1. Turn on your TV/radio. You will receive the latest weather updates and emergency instructions.
  • 2. Know where to go. You may need to reach higher ground quickly and on foot.
  • 3. Build or restock your emergency preparedness kit. Include a flashlight, batteries, cash, and first aid supplies.
  • 4. Prepare Your Home.
  • 5. Bring in outdoor furniture and move important indoor items to the highest possible floor.
  • 6. Disconnect electrical appliances and do not touch electrical equipment. if you are wet or standing in water, you could be electrocuted.
  • 7. If instructed, turn off your gas and electricity at the main switch or valve. This helps prevent fires and explosions.

  • 1. Identify places to go.
  • 2. Identify alternative travel routes that are not prone to flooding.
  • 3. Plan what to do with your pets.
  • 4. Fill your cars gas tank.
  • 5. If told to leave, do so quickly.

Take care of your family!
  • 1. Discuss flood plans with your family.
  • 2. Decide where you will meet if separated.
  • 3. Designate a contact person who can be reached if family members get separated.
  • 4. Make sure every family member has the contact information.

What to do when you get a flood warning in your area?
  • 1. Move immediately to higher ground or stay on high ground.
  • 2. Evacuate if directed.
  • 3. Avoid walking or driving through flood waters.
  • 4. Turn Around, Dont Drown!
  • 5. Flash floods develop quickly. Do not wait until you see rising water.
  • 6. Get out of low areas subject to flooding.
  • 7. If driving, do not drive through flooded roadways!

What to do during a flood?

  • 1. Monitor your surroundings.
  • 2. Monitor NOAA Weather Radio, local television and battery-operated radio stations, or go to www.weather.gov.
  • 3. If flooding occurs, go to higher ground and avoid areas subject to flooding.
  • 4. If water rises in your home before you evacuate, go to the top floor, attic, or roof.
  • 5. Turn off all utilities at the main power switch and close the main gas valve if advised to do so.
  • 6. If you've come in contact with floodwaters, wash your hands with soap and disinfected water.
  • 7. Get out of low areas that may be subject to flooding.
  • 8. Stay away from power lines and electrical wires.
  • 9. Act quickly. Save yourself, not your belongings.
  • 10. Secure your home: lock all doors and windows.
  • 11. Shut off water, gas, and electrical services before leaving your home or workplace.
  • 12. If someone is drowning, throw the victim something to help them float, such as a spare tire, large ball, or foam ice chest.

What NOT to do during a flood?

  • 1. Never try to walk or swim through flowing water.
  • 2. Avoid already-flooded areas and do not attempt to cross flowing water.
  • 3. NEVER drive through flooded roadways. STOP! Turn Around Dont Drown.
  • 4. Dont drive unless you have to. If you must drive, travel with care.

What to DO after a flood?

  • 1. Return home only when authorities say it is safe.
  • 2. Be aware of areas where floodwaters have receded and watch out for debris.
  • 3. Avoid standing water as it may be electrically charged from underground or downed power lines.
  • 4. Photograph damage to your property for insurance purposes.
  • 5. Check for structural damage. Inspect foundations for cracks or other damage.
  • 6. Wear sturdy shoes.
  • 7. Keep the power off until an electrician has inspected the system for safety.

What NOT to do after a flood?

  • 1. Do not enter a building that has flooded until local building officials have inspected it for safety.
  • 2. Do not enter a building if it is still flooded or surrounded by floodwater.
  • 3. Do not visit disaster areas following a flood. Your presence may hamper urgent emergency response and rescue operations.
  • 4. Do not attempt to drive through areas that are still flooded.

What is a landslide?

A landslide, also known as a landslip, is a geological phenomenon that includes a wide range of ground movements, such as rockfalls, deep failure of slopes, and shallow debris flows.

  • 1. They also can travel several miles from their source, growing in size as they pick up trees, boulders, cars and other materials.
  • 2. Landslide problems can be caused by land mismanagement, particularly in mountain, canyon and coastal regions.
  • 3. In areas burned by forest and brush fires, a lower threshold of precipitation may initiate landslides.

Interesting Fact:The fastest landslide ever recorded went an amazing 186 miles an hour!

Types of Landslides!

1. Debris flow

Slope material that becomes saturated with water may develop into a debris flow or mud flow. The resulting slurry of rock and mud may pick up trees, houses and cars, thus blocking bridges and tributaries causing flooding along its path.

2. Earthflows

Earthflows are downslope, viscous flows of saturated, fine-grained materials, which move at any speed from slow to fast. Typically, they can move at speeds from 0.17 to 20 km/h (0.1 to 12.4 mph). Though these are a lot like mudflows, overall they are more slow moving and are covered with solid material carried along by flow from within.

3. Debris landslide

A debris slide is a type of slide characterized by the chaotic movement of rocks, soil, and debris mixed with water and/or ice. They are usually triggered by the saturation of thickly vegetated slopes which results in an incoherent mixture of broken timber, smaller vegetation and other debris.

4. Sturzstrom

A sturzstrom is a rare, poorly understood type of landslide, typically with a long run-out. Often very large, these slides are unusually mobile, flowing very far over a low angle, flat, or even slightly uphill terrain.

5. Shallow landslide

Landslide in which the sliding surface is located within the soil mantle or weathered bedrock (typically to a depth from few decimetres to some metres) is called a shallow landslide. They usually include debris slides, debris flow, and failures of road cut-slopes. Landslides occurring as single large blocks of rock moving slowly down slope are sometimes called block glides.

6. Deep-seated landslide

Landslides in which the sliding surface is mostly deeply located below the maximum rooting depth of trees (typically to depths greater than ten meters). Deep-seated landslides usually involve deep regolith, weathered rock, and/or bedrock and include large slope failure associated with translational, rotational, or complex movement.

Predicting a landslide!

  • 1. It is possible to predict the timing of a slope failure by interpreting the rate of deflection measured by extensometers placed across tension cracks of a slope.
  • 2. Failure predictions rely on extensometers placed across scarps, and areas will be considered "off-limits" when the rate of movement exceeds 2 to 4 mm/hour.

What to do before a landslide?

The following are things you can do to protect yourself, your family and your property from the effects of a landslide or debris flow:

  • 1. To begin preparing, you should build an emergency kit and make a family communications plan.
  • 2. Prepare for landslides by following proper land-use procedures - avoid building near steep slopes, close to mountain edges, near drainage ways or along natural erosion valleys.
  • 3. Learn whether debris flows have occurred in your area by contacting local officials. Slopes where debris flows have occurred in the past are likely to experience them in the future.
  • 4. Get a ground assessment of your property.
  • 5. Consult a professional for advice on appropriate preventative measures for your home or business, such as flexible pipe fittings, which can better resist breakage.
  • 6. Protect your property by planting ground cover on slopes and building retaining walls.
  • 7. In mudflow areas, build channels or deflection walls to direct the flow around buildings. Be aware, however, if you build walls to divert debris flow and the flow lands on a neighbor's property, you may be liable for damages.
  • 8. If you are at risk from a landslide talk to your insurance agent. Debris flow may be covered by flood insurance policies from the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP).

What to DO during a landslide?

During a severe storm, stay alert and awake. Many deaths from landslides occur while people are sleeping.

  • 1. Listen to local news stations on a battery-powered radio for warnings of heavy rainfall.
  • 2. Listen for unusual sounds that might indicate moving debris, such as trees cracking or boulders knocking together.
  • 3. Move away from the path of a landslide or debris flow as quickly as possible.
  • 4. Avoid river valleys and low-lying areas.
  • 5. Curl into a tight ball and protect your head if escape is not possible.
  • 6. If you are near a stream or channel, be alert for any sudden increase or decrease in water flow and notice whether the water changes from clear to muddy. Such changes may mean there is debris flow activity upstream so be prepared to move quickly.

What to DO after a landslide?

  • 1. Go to a designated public shelter if you have been told to evacuate or you feel it is unsafe to remain in your home. Text SHELTER + your ZIP code to 43362 (4FEMA) to find the nearest shelter in your area (example: shelter 12345).
  • 2. Stay away from the slide area. There may be danger of additional slides.
  • 3. Listen to local radio or television stations for the latest emergency information.
  • 4. Watch for flooding, which may occur after a landslide or debris flow. Floods sometimes follow landslides and debris flows because they may both be started by the same event.
  • 5. Check for injured and trapped persons near the slide, without entering the direct slide area. Direct rescuers to their locations.
  • 6. Look for and report broken utility lines and damaged roadways and railways to appropriate authorities. Reporting potential hazards will get the utilities turned off as quickly as possible, preventing further hazard and injury.
  • 7. Check the building foundation, chimney, and surrounding land for damage. Damage to foundations, chimneys, or surrounding land may help you assess the safety of the area.
  • 8. Replant damaged ground as soon as possible since erosion caused by loss of ground cover can lead to flash flooding and additional landslides in the near future.
  • 9. Seek advice from a geotechnical expert for evaluating landslide hazards or designing corrective techniques to reduce landslide risk. A professional will be able to advise you of the best ways to prevent or reduce landslide risk, without creating further hazard.

What is a Hailstorm?

Hail storms are defined as thunderstorms that produce hail which occurs when you have a cold air mass, high in the atmosphere, meets a warm, moist air mass or front. The warm air will cool rapidly and lose its water in the form of rain. If the cold air is cold enough or high enough in the atmosphere that rain will freeze and fall to the ground as ice.

  • 1. It can destroy the crops ready for harvest.
  • 2. Hail damage to roofs often goes unnoticed until further structural damage is seen
  • 3. Hailstorm is one of the most significant thunderstorm hazards to aircraft.

Interesting Fact: An average 'hailstone' can have diameter ranging between 5 to 150 millimeters!

Predicting a Hailstorm!

It is difficult to pin point where exactly a large hail shaft will strike just as it is difficult to predict where tornadoes will exactly occur. However, the general region where hail can be expected is very predictable. Hail occurs in association with thunderstorms, particularly supercell thunderstorms.

Below are factors to consider when trying to forecast for the likeliness and size of hail.

  • 1. Elevation: Higher elevation areas are closer to the cold layers of the upper atmosphere.
  • 2. Freezing Level: The freezing level determines the depth of the atmosphere that is above freezing.
  • 3. Wet Bulb Zero Level: The wet bulb zero level is defined as the freezing level that will result due to evaporative cooling.
  • 4. Convective available Potential Energy (CAPE): This is the most important factor in determining hail size. CAPES under 1000 J/kg generally produce borderline severe hail (near 3/4" or less) while CAPES over 2000 J/kg can produce very large hailstones.
  • 5. Supercell Thunderstorm: Strong upper level winds allow CAPE to be maximized to its fullest potential. Strong upper level winds tilt the updraft of developing thunderstorms. This allows the updraft and downdraft to be separated from each other. This produces higher UVV's in the updraft.
  • 6. Precipitable Level: The weight of moisture and water will influence the strength of the updraft. High moisture soundings result in water loading. CAPE is reduced with water loading since the force of gravity pushes down on the liquid water drops.

What to do before a Hailstorm?

What you need to DO to be ready to face hailstorm?

  • 1. Keep an eye on the weather before a hailstorm strikes. While it is difficult to accurately predict a hailstorm, they are most commonly associated with severe weather such as tornadoes and thunderstorms.
  • 2. If hail has been predicted, park your vehicle inside your garage to protect it.
  • 3. Close your drapes, blinds or window shades to prevent potential injury from broken glass blowing inside.
  • 4. If you have trees near your home, they should be trimmed regularly. Keep an eye out for any branches that could potentially fall on your home during a storm.
  • 5. Create and practice a plan of action for your family. Discuss where and how you will seek shelter during a storm, ensure that everyone is aware of the location of first-aid kits and fire extinguishers, and choose a place for your family to meet if you get separated.
  • 6. If your roof is aging, consider replacing it before hail season begins.
  • 7. Stay tuned to local radio and TV stations for important weather updates.

What to DO during a Hailstorm?

  • 1. Stay away from skylights, windows and doors.
  • 2. During a hailstorm, find shelter and avoid underpasses or lowlands that are prone to flooding.
  • 3. Protect yourself if you are driving. Motorists should pull off to the side of the road, away from trees or other areas where debris could fall, and stay undercover until the storm has passed.
  • 4. Take shelter indoors. Get inside a sturdy house or other building. As soon as you hear thunder and lightning, the threat for hail is possible.

What to NOT to do during a Hailstorm?

  • 1. Do not try to go outside to protect your property during a storm. Stay indoors until the storm has passed.
  • 2. Do not use phones or touch metallic objects like stoves, radiators, metal pipes, and sinks.

  • 2. Check power-lines and make certain none are downed at your house or lying near water.
  • 3. Review your insurance policy contend with local insurance agent or the company.
  • 4. Questions neighbors about any damage to their property.
  • 4. Check with local TV about area damage.
